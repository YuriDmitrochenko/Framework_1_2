## About This Work

Cancer research has traditionally focused on the active components of disease: proliferating cells, driver mutations, signaling pathways, immune escape, and clonal evolution. Framework 1.2 approaches oncogenesis from a complementary direction, asking a question the mutational model leaves largely unaddressed: how does a malignant clone persist for years or decades despite continuously losing the very cells that define it?

The monograph develops a single organizing hypothesis — that every long-lived biological system depends on a protected **functional reserve** (a stem-cell niche, a satellite-cell compartment, a granuloma, an immunological memory pool), and that oncogenesis is best understood not as the invention of a new biological mechanism, but as the **progressive capture of an already-existing reserve mechanism by an evolving clone**. The central question the book asks, in place of “why do cancer cells proliferate?”, is *“who owns the reserve?”*

This is not presented as a finished theory. Several chapters deliberately test the model against biological systems chosen specifically because the model’s assumptions should fail there — lymphoid tissue, non-clonal granulomas, and gestational choriocarcinoma. In each case the framework was either modified or made to state its own boundary explicitly, rather than being forced into agreement with the evidence. The monograph is offered as a research map: a set of literature-grounded, testable hypotheses intended for experimental and clinical scrutiny, not as a closed conclusion.

The work was produced through a sustained “BI + AI” collaboration between the author and AI research assistants (Claude, Anthropic; ChatGPT, OpenAI), used throughout for literature exploration, critical analysis, and the organization of interdisciplinary material — see the Afterword for full acknowledgments.

This monograph continues the author’s earlier work, **Framework 1.0** (*the living cell as a hydrogel system; tissue organization and oncogenesis; the Regeneration Pendulum*) and **Framework 1.1** (*practical applications to aging and cancer*).

## How to Read This Series

- Each numbered **Article** and **Companion** piece is self-contained: it opens with an Abstract, develops its argument in numbered sections with literature citations, and closes with **Testable Predictions** and an explicit **Limitations** section.
- Boundary-case articles (Lymphomas, Granulomas, Choriocarcinoma) are written to stress-test the model, not merely illustrate it. Where the model’s transfer to a new tissue is strained rather than supported, this is stated directly in the text rather than smoothed over.
- Cross-references between articles are given by article number (e.g., “Article 4 §7”) so that individual chapters can be read out of order.
